## 1. Why Working Backwards Exists

The Working Backwards process was created at Amazon to keep product development focused on
customer needs. The core insight: writing a press release is a forcing function. When you write a
press release, you describe your product the way a customer will encounter it. You aren't thinking
about your technical capabilities, your org chart, or your competitive positioning. You're thinking
about whether a customer reading this announcement would be compelled to try the product.

The FAQ section then works backward from that customer-facing vision to identify every problem
that needs to be solved to make it real: technical, financial, legal, operational, and organizational.

Most PR/FAQs at Amazon never become products. That is the point. Spending time upfront to
think through all the details of a product, without committing engineering resources, preserves
resources for the ideas that survive the process.

The PR/FAQ process is a funnel, not a tunnel. A funnel accepts many ideas and progressively
eliminates lower-potential ones. A tunnel pushes every idea through to launch. The PR/FAQ
process is designed to be a funnel.

## 2. PR/FAQ Components in Detail

### Press Release Structure

**Headline**: Name the product so the target customer will understand. One sentence under the
title that is clear and specific.

**Subheading**: Describe the customer and the benefit they will gain. One sentence. Being
precise about the customer is critical. "Everyone" is not a customer segment. Great products
are tailored to the specific needs of a specific customer segment.

**Summary Paragraph**: Start with the city, media outlet (if applicable), and proposed launch
date. Summarize the product and its benefits. The launch date should be realistic. In early
drafts it can be a rough estimate; by the final draft it should be the planned launch date.

**Problem Paragraph**: Describe the problem the product solves, written from the customer's
point of view. This requires a deep understanding of customer needs. Not all problems are
created equal. You must identify a problem with a large enough total addressable market (TAM).
The TAM is the number of customers who have this problem multiplied by what each is willing
to pay to solve it. If the population of potential customers is small, or customers won't pay
enough to solve the problem, it's not worth pursuing.

**Solution Paragraph(s)**: Describe the product in detail and how it solves the customer's
problem. Three requirements:
1. The solution must address the problem directly. If the problem is organic grocery delivery
   between 6-8am twice weekly, the solution must be about that, not about personalized fruit
   recommendations.
2. The solution must be described with enough detail to be clear about how it addresses the
   problem. Vague, high-level descriptions are not useful.
3. The solution must acknowledge competition. What do customers use today? How is this
   meaningfully differentiated? Include language like: "Today, customers use X, Y, or Z to
   meet their needs. Those products fall short because [specific reasons]. This product
   addresses those gaps by [specific ways]."

If your proposed solution solves the same problems in the same ways as existing products,
go back to the drawing board.

**Spokesperson Quote**: One quote from a company spokesperson. Should reinforce the
customer benefit and the company's commitment.

**Customer Quote**: One quote from a hypothetical customer describing how the product
benefits them. Should feel authentic and specific, not like marketing copy.

**Getting Started**: Describe how easy it is to get started. Provide a link or next step
where customers can learn more.

### Total PR Length
The press release should be a few paragraphs, always less than one page (roughly 400-600
words). There are no awards for extra pages. The goal is distilled thinking, not a demonstration
of all the work you've done.

## 3. The FAQ Question Bank

### External FAQ (Customer/Press Questions)

These are the questions that customers, press, and the general public would ask. The answers
should be written in language customers can understand. No corporate jargon.

Standard external questions to consider:
- What is the price?
- How does it work?
- How do I get help or customer support?
- Where can I buy/access it?
- What is the warranty or service guarantee?
- What is the return/cancellation policy?
- How do I install or set it up?
- What are the system requirements or prerequisites?
- Is it compatible with [relevant existing products/services]?
- When will it be available?
- What data do you collect and how is it protected?

### Internal FAQ (Leadership/Stakeholder Questions)

These are the questions senior leaders and cross-functional stakeholders will ask. This section
demonstrates mastery of every aspect of the product. It should be optimistic but realistic.
It should include data-based assessments, not assumptions presented as facts.

Standard internal questions to consider:

**Customer and Market**
- What products or solutions does the target customer use today to solve this problem?
- What problem(s) does this product solve for customers?
- How does this product create value for customers? In what ways is it better, cheaper,
  or faster than alternatives?
- Who are the current competitors?
- How large is estimated consumer demand? What is the TAM?
- How many consumers have this need or problem?
- How many are willing to spend money to solve it, and how much?
- How many have the characteristics/capabilities necessary to use the product?

**Product and Technical**
- What happens if a customer encounters [edge case]? How does the product handle it?
- What are the challenging technical problems that need to be solved?
- What new capabilities will we need to build or acquire?
- What third-party dependencies exist? Why would they partner with us?
- What third-party technologies must function properly for this to work?

**Business and Financial**
- What are the per-unit economics? Expected gross profit and contribution profit per unit?
- How much upfront investment is required (people, technology, inventory, infrastructure)?
- How will we manage the risk of upfront investment?
- Based on investment and per-unit economics, how long until profitability?
- What assumptions must be true for this to succeed?

**Risk and Constraints**
- What are the top three reasons this product will not succeed?
- Are there regulatory or legal issues to consider?
- What needs to be true for this product to be successful?
- What are the conditions under which this product fails?

**Operations and Organization**
- What organizational changes are needed to support this product?
- What is the staffing plan?
- How will we measure success? What are the key metrics?
- What is the timeline from approval to launch?

## 4. The Review Process

The PR/FAQ review process is not about selling an idea. It is about truth-seeking and
improvement.

**Solo drafting**: The first draft is typically written by a product manager or the person who
originated the idea. Early drafts will have holes and unanswered questions. The author
researches to fill gaps, and detail increases with each draft.

**Small group review**: Once a draft is ready for feedback, the author shares it with their
manager and a small cross-functional group (roughly 10 people). The document is distributed
at the start of the meeting. Everyone reads silently for 15-20 minutes and adds comments.
The remaining 40 minutes are for questions, debate, and discussion. Senior attendees speak
last to avoid anchoring others.

**Executive review**: Once the small group is satisfied, the document goes to executive
leadership. Multiple subsequent drafts and review meetings typically follow for ideas under
serious consideration.

**Post-meeting**: The author distributes meeting minutes with notes on feedback, then
revises the document incorporating responses. This cycle repeats.

The process creates a tier of skilled evaluators. The more PR/FAQs leaders read and the more
products they build using this process, the better they become at identifying flaws in thinking.

## 5. Common Mistakes

**Confusing speed with velocity**: People resist spending weeks or months on PR/FAQs before
building. But speed without direction is not useful. Velocity is speed with direction. Investing
time upfront in planning means moving faster during production because the critical decisions
are already made and documented.

**Selling instead of truth-seeking**: The goal of a PR/FAQ review is not to get approval. It is
to identify what would prevent the product from succeeding and to find solutions. Ask: "What
needs to be true for this to succeed?" and "What new approach have we developed to address
this barrier?"

**Discounting competition**: If the customer problem is real, customers already have some
way of dealing with it. Be honest about existing solutions and articulate clearly why customers
would switch.

**Skills-forward thinking**: Starting from what you can build today instead of what the
customer needs. The PR/FAQ process deliberately decouples "what customers want" from
"what we're currently good at building."

**Building a great product for the wrong problem**: A technically impressive solution that
doesn't solve a problem customers care about will fail. The Fire Phone is often cited as an
example. Get the problem right first.

**More is not better**: Early PR/FAQs often run too long because writers include everything
to avoid hard decisions about what matters. Restricting length is a forcing function that
develops better thinking and communication. Keep the PR under one page. Keep the FAQ
under five pages.

**Vague customer definitions**: "Everyone" or "businesses" is not a customer segment.
Define exactly who you are building for: their role, their context, their constraints, their
current behavior.

## 6. Decision Framework

When a PR/FAQ reaches a decision point, common outcomes include:

**Go**: Resources, timeline, and operational details are spelled out in the FAQ. An executive
stays close to the team with regular reviews.

**No-go for copycat**: The solution is not differentiated enough. Go back and find what
makes this meaningfully different.

**No-go for small TAM**: The market is too small. Revisit the customer segment, problem
definition, or solution to grow the addressable market.

**No-go for unfavorable economics**: Investment is high and payoff is risky or low. Find
ways to start smaller with fewer resources.

**No-go for unsolved problems**: Critical problems identified in the FAQ don't have solutions
yet. The PR/FAQ can be revisited in future quarters if solutions emerge.

**Deprioritized**: The idea has merit but other initiatives rank higher. It goes into the backlog
with a planned revisit date.
